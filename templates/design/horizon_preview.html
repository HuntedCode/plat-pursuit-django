Horizon Design Workshop

The treatment for the path forward.

Horizon is the glow on the edge of any unfinished progression — the far wall of the planning desk where the next stop is marked but not yet reached. Wherever progress is shown (XP bar, stage progression, tier completion, Pursuer Level toward next milestone, blueprint --pp-build mask), Horizon communicates there's more this way without literal arrows or "Click to continue" CTAs. The treatment lives in four forms — linear bar, stepped pips, radial arc, vertical fill — and inherits the brand's cyan accent on the cool end, shifting toward amber as completion approaches.

This workshop covers Horizon comprehensively (linear, stepped, radial, vertical) since progress indicators appear across the whole product, not just gamification. The team's decisions to converge on: form vocabulary, color progression aggressiveness, marker style, near-completion pulse intensity, Tally pairing rules.

01 The Horizon

The canonical form, all traits visible.

One linear bar at ~64% completion, sized at hero scale (12px tall). All four Horizon traits are visible: the cool-to-warm gradient, the leading-edge glow that bleeds beyond the fill, the faint marker at the next stop, and the warming band shift. 1 · Subtle gradient

Cool-leaning at the base of the fill, warming as it approaches the leading edge. The gradient is the doc's literal "subtle" — no rainbow, no drama.

2 · Leading-edge glow

The right edge bleeds beyond the fill into the dark track. The eye reads the glow as "the path keeps going," not "the fill is done."

3 · Color semantic

Warmer the closer to 100%, cooler the further. This bar is in the warming band — past the cool baseline, not yet at the hot "almost there" pulse.

4 · Faint marker

An optional tick just beyond the fill marks the next stop. Mood, not arrow — gentle enough to ignore, present enough to feel.

02 The Forms

One language. Six shapes.

The Horizon language translates across six canonical shapes — the four atomic forms (linear bar, stepped pips, radial arc, vertical fill) plus two composite forms for specific surfaces (segmented bar for tier ladders, vertical pips for stepped climbs). Each handles a different progress context but inherits the same color semantic, glow language, and pulse behavior. All six below sit in the same color band (warming, ~60–65% completion) so you can compare apples-to-apples.

Pips, segmented bar, and vertical pips now use position-stepped coloring (each done element gets a color from where it sits in the sequence, not just from the overall band) so the discrete forms carry the same internal cool→warm gradient story as the linear bar and vertical fill. The radial arc uses an SVG linearGradient with band-driven stop colors for the same reason.

Linear bar

XP · level · roadmap · completion

Where it lives: the canonical form. Any continuous-progression indicator. Job rows, Pursuer Level toward next milestone, roadmap completion, tier progression, content unlocking, sync progress.

Stepped pips

stages · checklist · multi-step

Where it lives: discrete-count progressions. Stage rosters in a badge series, checklist steps in onboarding, multi-step forms, milestone counts. The "next" pip (outlined, the leading edge) is the marker analogue.

Radial arc

hero · completion ring · status

Where it lives: compact circular contexts. Pursuer Level rings on a profile chip, a single-badge completion ring, a sync-status ring on the navbar, a campaign progress ring on a roadmap header.

Vertical fill

blueprint · being-built · ascent

Where it lives: bottom-up "being constructed" contexts. The Frame's blueprint --pp-build mask is already this form; the language unifies it. Useful for stage progression where the metaphor is climbing, not just filling.

Segmented bar

tier ladder · multi-section
Bronze Silver Gold Platinum

Where it lives: tier-ladder progressions (Bronze → Silver → Gold → Platinum), multi-section roadmaps, "you're in section N of M" contexts. Each completed segment carries position-stepped warmth; the current segment shows internal progress like a linear bar; future segments stay outlined.

Vertical pips

stepped climb · ladder rungs

Where it lives: stepped climb visualizations where the metaphor is ascent rather than a continuous fill. The vertical-pips form mirrors horizontal pips the same way the vertical-fill mirrors the linear bar. Useful for badge-stage rosters rendered as ladder rungs, or any "rung N of M" surface.

05 Variants & Interaction

The marker question, plus a live scrubber.

A — No marker

Just the gradient + glow. Cleanest read, but loses the "next stop" affordance.

A faint dot just beyond the fill. Reads as a destination without shouting.

07 Anti-patterns

What we are NOT.

The doc lists four specific anti-patterns for the Horizon. Each card below renders a deliberate violation with a "do-not-ship" stamp and a reason. The goal of this section is mutual recognition — when someone proposes a Horizon variant in code review, this section is the shared dictionary of "this drifted into a bad lane, fix it."

Do not ship

1. Literal arrows / chevrons

A blinking chevron beside the bar is too directive. The Horizon's job is to communicate "there's more this way" through mood — gradient + glow + warm shift — not a literal pointing finger. If a CTA-class arrow shows up next to a bar, the bar is failing.

›
Do not ship

2. Heavy dramatic gradients

The Horizon's gradient is a hint, not a flag. Rainbow gradients, hard purple-to-yellow ramps, neon stops — these belong in mobile-game UI (anti-ref #4). The shift is supposed to feel like sunlight warming, not a casino billboard.

Do not ship

3. "Click to continue" CTAs

The Horizon is mood, not a button. Bolting a CTA onto the bar collapses the primitive into a navigation control. If a CTA truly belongs in the surface, place it elsewhere — outside the Horizon's space — so the bar keeps doing its quiet "more this way" job without competing.

Do not ship

4. Decorative-only (no real %)

A Horizon that animates without ever tracking a real value is the worst kind of dishonest. The doc names it explicitly: must always pair with a real percentage, never decorative-only. The fill below loops 25→75→25 — pretty, also a lie.

08 Decision Criteria

Five tests. Pass all five.

Argue against the criteria, not the candidates.

1Translates across all four forms

Same language reads in linear, stepped, radial, and vertical. Fails when one form needs special rules to look right. See Section 2.

2Holds at every scale

Hero, row, inline, micro. Fails when the glow vanishes at micro or the gradient banding becomes visible at hero. See Section 4.

3The color semantic reads on first scan

A new user should feel "I'm close" vs "I just started" without reading any text. Fails when the warm/cool shift is too subtle (no signal) or too aggressive (anti-ref territory). See Section 3.

4Composes with Tally cleanly

In the canonical Job row, Tally is the protagonist and Horizon trails. Fails when the bar fights the number for attention. See Section 6.

5Passes the anti-refs

No literal arrows, no rainbow gradients, no CTAs, no decorative-only animations. See Section 7.

How to use this workshop

  • Sections 1–2 are the form vocabulary. Pick the four forms (linear / stepped / radial / vertical) as the canonical set, or argue to add / drop a form.
  • Section 3 is the color semantic. The cool → warming → warm → hot ramp is the load-bearing decision; calibrate aggressiveness here.
  • Section 4 is the scale survival check. If any form breaks at micro, decide the smallest scale we'll commit to.
  • Section 5 is the marker decision. Pick A, B, or C. The slider is for feel-the-bands gut-checking.
  • Section 6 is the canonical composition. Once Tally locks, this section reflects the picked face automatically.
  • Section 7 is shared vocabulary for code review. Quote a card by number when flagging a Horizon variant that drifted.
  • Section 8 is the rubric. Five tests, pass all five.
