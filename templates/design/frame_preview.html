Design Preview

The Frame

The proposed visual primitive for PlatPursuit badges. It's the chrome that surrounds every badge wherever it renders (gallery, share images, comments, profiles, everywhere). This preview shows how it looks across tiers, sizes, and states, plus an exploration of optional cosmetic effects (“flair”) we’re considering for a future customization layer.

Hover, click, and toggle the interactive controls in each section. Feedback prompts at the very bottom.

1. The four tiers

Bronze, Silver, Gold, and Platinum. Each tier carries through the whole card: title bar, art container, and metadata strip all share a tier palette so a Bronze card feels Bronze top to bottom, not Bronze art wrapped in neutral chrome.

2. Tier label placement: ribbon vs title bar

Early team feedback: the ribbon (top-right of the art) covers part of the badge. Tier identity already shows in three other places (backdrop art, corner notch color, chrome tint), so the ribbon's job is just the explicit "Gold / Platinum" label.

Proposed change: move the tier label out of the art and into the title bar, right-aligned next to the series name. Long series titles (e.g. "Yakuza / Like a Dragon Anthology") truncate with an ellipsis so the tier label stays anchored on the right.

6. Frame states

Three states a Frame can carry pre-Earned. The art layer never greyscales (greyscale reads “disabled”), tier identity is preserved across every state so the gallery never loses its color rhythm.

Unearned · dim is the current default lock state. Unearned · blueprint is an alternative treatment the visual-identity doc speculated about: render the badge as the architectural blueprint of what the Pursuer is working toward, not a greyed-out icon. The fourth state, Pinned, lives in its own dedicated exploration in Section 7.

D overshoots its target and snaps back, the most game-y read. E adds welding sparks that arc off the build line and fall under gravity, with the line itself glowing hot during the build.

The Earn Moment: the choreographed sequence that plays when the final stage clears. Heavy and deliberate, because these badges don’t come along often. Build line completes with welding sparks → card lifts off the page (and stays lifted) → a welding torch laps the perimeter, sealing the frame → sealed-border glow → horizontal scan beam sweeps bottom-to-top, generating the tier-tinted background → a sheen sweeps across the card as the “stamp of completion” → the front engraving slow-burns in → card flips to its back face → a vertical scan beam sweeps right-to-left, revealing the back content → card finally settles back down. The flip doubles as a teaching moment for the click-to-flip interaction.

7. Pinned exploration

The pinned state needs to read as “currently active” from across a gallery without competing with the tier color hierarchy. Four directions, ordered from lightest touch to fullest commitment. All examples on Gold Marvel Universe, 8 of 10 stages, so the only variable is the pinned treatment itself.

The map-pin chip is a small circular badge anchored at the top-left corner with a slight rotation so it reads as a physical pin pressed into the chrome. Accent color uses our brand primary cyan (luminous teal, hue ~206), decoupled from the tier color, so “pinned” becomes a wayfinding signal rather than another tier echo.

12. Key design choices

  • Trading-card layout. Series name at the top (small caps, like a card set subtitle), badge art in the middle, badge name + earn metadata at the bottom. Closer to a Topps baseball card than a Pokemon card. The badge name is the headline; everything else supports it.
  • Corner notches as brand mark. Inspired by graded trading-card sleeves. Small (7-10px depending on tier), tier-colored, and the same diamond shape across every Frame. The goal: a screenshot of any Frame should read as PlatPursuit before any text loads.
  • Tier-tinted everywhere. Title, art, and plinth all share a tier palette (Bronze warm, Silver cool, Gold warm, Platinum cool iridescent). The whole card reads as one cohesive tier color top to bottom, not chrome wrapped around a tier-colored art.
  • Rarity as a feature. The earn-percentage in the plinth is treated as a status indicator, not just a number. Common badges read muted; rare badges glow gold; mythic badges (sub-1%) have an animated pulse and sparkle icon. A mythic badge should stand out in a gallery at a glance, regardless of its tier.
  • Click to flip. Each Frame can show personal stats on the back (earn date, stages complete, rarity rank, next-tier progress). The trading-card metaphor goes from “looks like a card” to “behaves like a card.”
  • Flair = optional, not default. The base Frame is what every user sees by default. The flair effects in Sections 10 and 11 are explorations of a future customization layer where users could unlock and equip cosmetic effects. The base design needs to be strong on its own first.

13. Earn engraving (limited-run authentication)

The collector instinct, made literal. Every Pursuer’s frame carries their permanent earn-rank etched into the plinth, the way a limited-edition watch carries a serial on its case-back. The badge exists at many earn-ranks across the community; your frame’s engraving is yours forever.

No closing denominator (the community keeps earning, so “247 / 1,420” would shift every day). Just the rank, locked. A #5 owner can flex being the fifth ever; a #14,802 owner sees their place in the queue. The number is the charm hook and the brand authenticity mark in one stroke.

14. Feedback prompts

The honest gut-checks we’re looking for. Don’t worry about being polite. Conflicting opinions are useful.

  • 1. First impression. Does this read as PlatPursuit? Would you recognize a Frame in the wild as ours, or could it belong to any tracker?
  • 2. Tier identity. Do the four tiers feel distinct enough? Does the tier signaling (notches + chrome tint + ribbon + backdrop) feel right, or is it too loud / too quiet?
  • 3. The badge gallery experience. Section 9 simulates a gallery view. Try the background toggle. Does it hold up at scale? Would you enjoy scrolling through your own badge collection rendered like this?
  • 4. Card flip (Section 5). Is the click-to-flip useful, or unnecessary chrome? What would you most want to see on the back?
  • 5. Frame states (Sections 6 & 7). Two questions stacked: (a) Unearned dim vs. incremental blueprint build — which sells “working toward this” better? (b) For Pinned, which of A/B/C/D feels right? Tier-coupled (A/B) is quieter; accent-decoupled (C/D) creates a system-wide “currently active” signature in our brand cyan.
  • 6. Earn engraving (Section 13). The limited-run authentication idea: every Pursuer’s frame carries their permanent earn-rank engraved into the plinth. Does it land? Which engraving format (minimal “Earn #247”, decorated “#247 of all-time”, or with-stamp) feels right? Would you brag about your number?
  • 7. Flair effects (Sections 10 & 11). Which ones land? Which feel like “too much”? Which would you want for your own badge? Are there missing categories (e.g. nature, weather, magic, sci-fi) you’d expect to see?
  • 8. Deal-breakers. Is there anything in this design you’d push back on hard? Anything that feels off-brand for PlatPursuit?
  • 9. The unknown unknowns. Something you’d expect this design to address that it doesn’t? Edge cases we missed?

This is an interactive HTML preview, not a final implementation. Real badge artwork, real user data, real performance characteristics will all behave somewhat differently when integrated into PlatPursuit proper. Treat the visuals as directional.
